\begin{abstract}
Training a reinforcement-learning (RL) agent for an imperfect-information card game runs into the
\emph{opponent bottleneck}: a policy is only as good as the opponents it practises against. We
study this in Gin Rummy by building a complete framework---an action-masked PPO/TRPO learner, a
hand-coded \emph{gold-standard} (provably optimal) opponent used purely as a yardstick, a
distributed master--worker stack that lets a 7B language model (LLM) act inside the game, an
opponent-curriculum system, and a browser game---then benchmarking nearly every plausible way to
strengthen the agent on a single objective: win-rate against the perfect player. Across $100+$
controlled experiments we find that (i) the gold standard beats every learned agent ($70$--$99\%$)
yet \emph{gins under $2\%$ of the time}, winning by low-deadwood knocking, and an RL agent
rediscovers this even when gin is rewarded $3\times$ more than a knock; (ii) imitation learning
(DAgger), dense short-term reward shaping, and live LLM-in-the-loop training all fail to beat plain
curriculum RL---each for an identifiable reason; and (iii) the levers that \emph{do} help---a
trust-region algorithm (TRPO), a knock-oriented reward, a rising opponent curriculum,
warm-starting, and keeping the best checkpoint---stack to push a lightweight agent from the
$\sim\!30\%$ self-play champion to $\mathbf{34\%}$ against perfect play. We report the successful
engineering together with a critical, honestly-negative analysis of why standard hybrid methods do
not surpass pure RL here, and argue the remaining gap needs search/planning or full-history memory
rather than further reward tuning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Reinforcement learning excels at sequential decision-making but is data-hungry and, crucially,
\emph{bootstraps off its opponents}: train against a weak player and the policy learns to exploit
weakness rather than to play well. Strong opponents are exactly what is scarce---hand-built solvers
are game-specific, and humans do not scale. Large language models (LLMs) are an appealing shortcut:
they bring broad, common-sense strategy zero-shot. This project asks, in the concrete testbed of
Gin Rummy, \textbf{how close a small, fast RL agent can get to optimal play, and which training
ideas actually move it there.}

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} (1) A reusable framework coupling masked-PPO/TRPO RL, a
\emph{provably optimal} benchmark agent, a distributed LLM-serving stack, an opponent curriculum,
and a web game (Sec.~2). (2) A gold-standard benchmark and a clean empirical finding: the optimal
policy almost never gins, and RL rediscovers this regardless of reward (Sec.~3, 4.6). (3) A
comparative study of $\sim\!10$ training regimes on one yardstick---win-rate vs.\ the perfect
player---with the \emph{reason} each succeeds or fails (Sec.~4, Fig.~\ref{fig:regimes}), including
honest negatives for DAgger, dense rewards, learned embeddings, and live LLM-in-the-loop. (4) A
recipe stacking the helpful levers to reach $34\%$ vs.\ perfect play (Sec.~4.7, 5).

\section{The Framework}
\textbf{Environment and learner.} We use PettingZoo/RLCard \texttt{gin\_rummy\_v4}; the observation
is four $52$-card planes (hand, top discard, known picks, rest). Our \texttt{MaskedGinRummyPolicy}
sets illegal-action logits to $-\infty$ so the agent only ever selects a legal move; agents act by
taking the highest-probability \emph{legal} action (no random fallback). \textbf{Gold standard.} A
hand-coded optimal player computes exact best melds via RLCard's solver and knocks the moment it
should; it is the yardstick and \emph{never} trains the RL agent. \textbf{Distributed LLM.} A single
rollout fires tens of thousands of opponent queries; at $0.5$--$3$\,s per 7B call a naive loop takes
hours. A master node load-balances over self-registering GPU workers behind a suit-symmetry cache,
speaking the unchanged Ollama API. A key infrastructure result: staging 7B weights on scratch/BeeGFS
rather than home NFS cuts worker load from $\sim\!28$\,min to $\sim\!27$\,s ($\mathbf{62\times}$),
and a $14$-worker pool sustains $\sim\!32$ queries/s. \textbf{Curriculum + game.} A scheduler ramps
opponents (random $\rightarrow$ past selves $\rightarrow$ strong models); a zero-dependency browser
client lets humans play any agent. Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows how the pieces connect.

\section{The Gold-Standard Benchmark and a Surprise}
To grade everything on one honest scale we built a provably optimal player and ran it against the
whole zoo of trained agents (Fig.~\ref{fig:gold}, $600$ games each). It wins $70.2\%$ vs.\ the
self-play champion and $70$--$99\%$ vs.\ all others. The surprise is the mechanism: the optimal
player \textbf{gins only $0.7$--$1.7\%$ of the time}, despite gin scoring the most points. It wins
by \emph{knocking early with low deadwood}. This inverts a natural intuition---chasing gin is a
beginner's trap---and reframes every reward experiment below.

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.84\linewidth]{gold_bench.png}
\caption{The optimal player beats every learned agent (left) yet gins under $2\%$ of the time
(right): it wins by low-deadwood knocking, not by chasing gin.}\label{fig:gold}
\end{figure}

\section{What We Tried: A Comparative Study}
We benchmarked nearly every reasonable training regime on the same metric---best win-rate vs.\ the
perfect player (Fig.~\ref{fig:regimes})---and, for each, identify \emph{why} it lands where it does.

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.76\linewidth]{regimes.png}
\caption{Every regime we benchmarked, ranked by measured win-rate against the perfect player.}
\label{fig:regimes}
\end{figure}

\subsection{RL backbone and reward shaping}
Action-masked PPO saturates against random opponents: an eight-configuration sweep lands every run
in a $98.3$--$99.6\%$ band (indistinguishable at $1000$-game eval), and a broader effort of $40+$
reward-shaping runs shows the gin/knock reward ratio is a real behavioural knob---a knock-biased
reward yields $97\%$ knocks (win $99.4\%$) while a gin-biased one yields $22\%$ gins (win $98.3\%$).
\emph{Reason:} random play is too weak to teach strategy, so the easy metric saturates; the reward
ratio sets the agent's risk appetite. This motivates stronger opponents and a principled reward.

\subsection{Self-play and curriculum}
Self-play is a free rising curriculum: a $3$M-step self-play agent beats its own progenitor $61\%$.
But an unguided pool of past selves \emph{diverged} after $\sim\!10$M steps. \emph{Reason:}
self-play creates difficulty for free but can chase non-transitive cycles; the schedule and
opponent mix matter, motivating the controlled sweep of Sec.~4.6.

\subsection{Why the hybrid methods fail (honest negatives)}
\textbf{Live LLM-in-the-loop.} Mid-size LLMs (Qwen2.5-7B, gpt-oss-20b) play real Gin Rummy with a
chain-of-thought prompt ($100\%$ legal moves) and even beat our self-play agent $3$--$2$ in short
matches---but at $9$--$27$\,s/move they are orders of magnitude too slow for the millions of moves
RL needs; even with the distributed stack, online training would take weeks. Vision-LLMs failed
outright ($\sim\!10\%$ legal moves): it is a text-reasoning task. \textbf{Imitation (DAgger).}
Cloning an expert move-by-move collapses to near-zero win-rate. \emph{Reason:} the student mimics
actions in familiar states but never internalises the \emph{reasoning} (e.g.\ tracking the
opponent), so it fails off-distribution---``mimicking a move'' $\neq$ ``understanding strategy.''
\textbf{Dense/short-term rewards.} Rewarding each step (horizon $L\!=\!2,5$) induces myopia:
$L\!=\!2$ shows no learning and $L\!=\!5$ saturates after $\sim\!500$k steps, ``farming'' instant
points rather than trying to win. \emph{Reason:} dense shaping distracts from the true sparse
objective. The redline that follows: we use the LLM/expert only to \emph{inform design}, never to
supervise the policy directly.

\subsection{Algorithm: PPO vs.\ TRPO}
Under identical masking, curriculum, and reward, TRPO beats PPO at $2$M steps on every opponent and
both seeds: $50.5\%$ vs.\ $31.0\%$ champion and $22.5\%$ vs.\ $15.0\%$ gold (Fig.~\ref{fig:algo}a).
\emph{Reason:} TRPO's trust-region update is more robust to the sparse-terminal, non-stationary
self-play target than PPO's fixed clip. (GRPO/DPO are LLM-alignment objectives with no per-move
environment analogue and do not apply.)

\subsection{State representation}
We compressed the large sparse observation into a dense $20$-d embedding two ways---self-supervised
temporal similarity (states near in a game are similar) and an LLM judging strategic similarity---
and used each (frozen) as the policy input. Both \emph{lose} to the raw sparse input vs.\ gold
($6$--$14\%$ vs.\ $15\%$). \emph{Reason:} a frozen bottleneck good at ``are these states similar''
still discards detail the policy needs; size and un-freezing did not recover it.

\subsection{The curriculum sweep and the key finding}
A $30$-run one-factor sweep over algorithm $\times$ reward $\times$ curriculum lands every recipe
near champion strength ($\sim\!30$--$33\%$ vs.\ gold), but yields one clean, sturdy result
(Fig.~\ref{fig:algo}b): \textbf{the gin-rate stays under $1\%$ regardless of reward}---even paying
$3\times$ for a gin does not raise it above the balanced baseline. \emph{Reason:} like the optimal
player, the policy discovers that chasing gin loses; you cannot bribe it into the bad habit. The
reward that actually helps does the opposite---a small ``knock earlier'' penalty that yields the
shortest games and the best play. Fig.~\ref{fig:learn} shows the mechanism: win-rate against the
champion climbs as the curriculum swaps in tougher opponents (random $\rightarrow$ pool
$\rightarrow$ self $\rightarrow$ strong), and the late dip on one run is exactly the drift that
motivates keep-the-best (Sec.~4.7).

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.64\linewidth]{learning_curves.png}
\caption{Skill (win-rate vs.\ the champion) rises through the curriculum stages; the late dip
motivates best-checkpoint selection.}\label{fig:learn}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Keep-the-best and warm-start (the recipe)}
A final series stacks the helpful levers: \emph{warm-start} from the champion, the knock-oriented
reward plus a dense \emph{deadwood-reduction} signal (a policy-invariant nudge toward the optimal
style, computed from the agent's own public hand), a curriculum seeded with the strongest prior
agents, and---critically---\emph{keeping the best checkpoint} (training drifts past its peak, so we
evaluate every $1$M steps and ship the best). Re-evaluated at $2000$ games, the strongest agent
reaches $\mathbf{34.2\% \pm 2.1\%}$ vs.\ gold and $51.4\%$ vs.\ the old champion---our best, up from
the champion's $\sim\!30\%$.

\section{What Mattered, and the Bottom Line}
\begin{table}[h]\centering\footnotesize

Idea & Verdict & Why \\\midrule
Keep the best checkpoint & \yes & training drifts past its peak; saving the best recovers $2$--$3$ pts \\
Warm-start from the champion & \yes & start strong, then specialise \\
TRPO over PPO & \yes & safer policy steps suit sparse, shifting self-play \\
Reward knocking, not gin & \yes & copies the optimal low-risk style \\
Rising opponent curriculum & \yes & always a fair-but-harder challenge \\
Pay more for gin & \textcolor{bad}{no effect} & the agent refuses the bad habit at any bribe \\
Learned state embeddings & \no & a frozen bottleneck discards useful detail \\
Imitation (DAgger) & \no & copies moves, not the reasoning behind them \\
Dense short-term rewards & \no & myopia---greedy for points, blind to winning \\
Live LLM-in-the-loop & \no & strong but far too slow for millions of moves \\
\bottomrule\end{tabular}

Stacking the helpful levers took a lightweight agent from the champion's $\sim\!30\%$ to $34\%$
against \emph{perfect} play (Fig.~\ref{fig:journey}) and produced one reusable scientific result
(optimal play rarely gins). The honest ceiling is the mid-$30$s percent: reward, algorithm, and
opponent tuning plateau there.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
We built the framework, the benchmark, and the comparative study to answer---fairly---how strong a
lightweight RL agent can be in Gin Rummy and what makes it so. Pure curriculum RL, sharpened with a
trust-region algorithm, a knock-oriented reward, warm-starting, and best-checkpoint selection, is
the winner; the popular hybrid shortcuts (imitation, dense reward, live LLM-in-the-loop) each fail
for a concrete, diagnosed reason. Surpassing the mid-$30$s ceiling against optimal play almost
certainly requires a \emph{different class} of method---search/planning such as
counterfactual-regret minimization (which powers superhuman poker), or an agent with explicit
full-history memory---rather than further reward or curriculum tuning. The framework, the
gold-standard benchmark, and the distributed LLM dataset pipeline are the reusable substrate for
that next step (e.g.\ offline RL on a large LLM-vs-LLM corpus). All figures regenerate from saved
JSON; live training curves are on Weights \& Biases.
